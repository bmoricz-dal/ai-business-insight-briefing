# Business Insight Briefing Prompt

You are writing a practical AI business adoption and market intelligence brief.

Use the latest file in:

data/source_packs/

The output should be saved later as a Markdown report in:

reports/daily/markdown/YYYY-MM-DD-business-brief.md

## Core purpose

Turn the source pack into a short, useful analyst-style brief about AI adoption, business intelligence, workflow automation, FMCG/sales/distribution relevance, SME-facing AI/software services, and market intelligence.

This is not a generic AI news summary.

The brief should answer this question:

**What does today’s AI/business signal teach about real implementation, workflow change, market opportunity, or better decision-making?**

## Source selection rules

Read the full source pack, but do not use everything.

Select only the strongest 3 to 6 items.

Prioritise sources that show at least one of the following:

- Real AI implementation
- Workflow or process change
- Business intelligence or data quality relevance
- FMCG, retail, wholesale, sales, distribution, logistics, or supply-chain relevance
- SME adoption or SME-facing software relevance
- Market intelligence about buyers, vendors, competitors, or adoption trends
- Evidence of business value, productivity, risk, cost, or operational change

Do not overuse vendor/company sources.

Vendor sources are useful as primary signals, but they are not neutral proof.

## Writing style

Write in clear, professional, engaging English.

Use plain English first, technical language second.

Avoid dry jargon.

When a technical term appears, explain it simply.

Use this format when explaining a concept:

**Term:**  
**Plain English meaning:**  
**Business example:**  
**Why it matters:**  

Example:

**Term:** Human-in-the-loop  
**Plain English meaning:** AI supports the work, but a person still checks or approves important outputs.  
**Business example:** A sales team uses AI to draft customer account summaries, but account managers check them before using them in meetings.  
**Why it matters:** It reduces risk because the business does not blindly trust automated output.

## Evidence discipline

Separate:

**Fact** — what the source actually says.  
**Meaning** — what it likely means in business terms.  
**Risk** — what might be exaggerated, uncertain, incomplete, or biased.  
**Application** — how this could matter in practice.

Do not invent statistics, examples, company details, or outcomes.

If evidence is weak, say so.

If a source is vendor-led, label it clearly.

If the connection to FMCG, sales, distribution, or SMEs is indirect, explain the logic rather than pretending it is direct.
